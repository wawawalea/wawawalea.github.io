About

I'm a senior at New York University studying Computer Science, Mathematics, and Philosophy.

I'm drawn to a basic question: when we study alignment, pre-training, and post-training, what are we actually studying? Which of the things we talk about are real, and how do they depend on or cause one another? I like research that takes a vague intuition and turns it into a claim you can actually test.

The tagline above is a small philosophy joke, but it points at something I really care about: how the study of LLMs and other intelligent neural networks can be made genuinely scientific, so that our assumptions about AI can be tested against evidence rather than just asserted.

Research

My research started with implementing and developing reinforcement learning algorithms. Building and running the agent systems around them gave me a close, hands-on view of how these models actually behave, and that's what pulled me toward mechanistic interpretability, and toward asking not just whether a post-training method works, but why it works, in precise mathematical terms.

I am currently working on two threads:

Causal study of LLM scheming. A causal study of when and why language models "scheme," that is, quietly pursue goals other than the ones we gave them, and what inside the model that behavior actually depends on.

Efficient LLM post-training. Designing training methods and pipelines that let a model pick up new skills without retraining it from scratch every time, turning a careful, mathematical understanding of post-training into systems that actually hold up at scale.
